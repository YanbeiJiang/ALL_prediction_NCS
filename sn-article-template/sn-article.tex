%% Abstract

\abstract{%
Acute lymphoblastic leukaemia (ALL) is the most prevalent malignancy
in children, and although contemporary treatment achieves five-year
survival rates exceeding 90\%, approximately 15--20\% of patients
relapse after apparent remission, with markedly worse prognosis upon
re-induction. Existing risk-stratification approaches rely primarily on
single-timepoint diagnostic features and fail to integrate the
longitudinal nature of clinical data acquisition or to prospectively
predict future adverse events.
Here we present a temporally-structured multimodal prediction framework
applied to a cohort of 301 paediatric ALL patients (2015--2019,
Chinese single-centre).
We define twelve feature sets~($\mathcal{F}_1 \subseteq \cdots
\subseteq \mathcal{F}_{12}$) whose incremental composition strictly
follows the clinical data-acquisition timeline, from white blood cell
count and age at Day~0 to minimal residual disease (MRD) measurements
at Day~46.
A two-phase system is evaluated: Phase~1 deploys four large language
models (LLMs) in a zero-shot setting to probe the upper bound of
prediction without training, while Phase~2 trains decision trees and
random survival forests on a dynamic case database that grows with
annual patient cohorts, explicitly simulating real-world hospital
deployment.
On the primary risk-stratification task (Final Risk), Phase~2 achieves
an AUROC of 0.949 using features available by Day~46, with
interpretable decision paths provided by the decision tree.
For survival prediction, the best model attains a concordance index of
0.746 and a time-dependent AUC at 24~months of 0.775 for
event-free survival (EFS).
A retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) component retrieves the $K=5$
most similar training cases by cosine similarity and applies a
similarity-weighted belief revision to the RF predicted probability,
yielding quantitative improvements on tasks where the feature space
clusters meaningfully (D46MRD AUROC: 0.655$\to$0.881 on Split~3),
while also supporting clinically interpretable LLM-generated narratives.
This framework demonstrates that temporally stratified multimodal
learning, aligned with clinical workflows, enables effective prediction
at every decision node of paediatric ALL management.
}

\keywords{paediatric acute lymphoblastic leukaemia, temporal feature
design, large language models, risk stratification, survival analysis,
retrieval-augmented generation}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
%%==============================================================%%

Acute lymphoblastic leukaemia (ALL) is the most common childhood
malignancy, accounting for approximately 25\% of all paediatric
cancers~\cite{[CITE:Hunger2015]}. Advances in risk-adapted chemotherapy
regimens and supportive care have elevated five-year event-free
survival (EFS) rates above 90\% in high-income settings; however, the
15--20\% of patients who relapse face substantially worse outcomes,
with long-term survival dropping to 30--50\% following
re-induction~\cite{[CITE:Bhojwani2013],[CITE:Pui2008]}.
Critically, the majority of relapses occur after apparent clinical
remission, making prospective identification of high-risk patients
a central yet unsolved challenge in paediatric oncology.
Current standard-of-care risk stratification (Initial Risk at
diagnosis; Final Risk after Day~46 induction assessment) relies on
pre-specified clinical cut-offs for age, white blood cell count,
cytogenetics, immunophenotype, and MRD, but these categorical rules do
not fully exploit the rich multimodal information accumulated
progressively during treatment, nor do they yield personalised
probability estimates for future relapse or survival.

Computational approaches have been applied to ALL outcome prediction
with increasing frequency. Machine learning models trained on flow
cytometry profiles~\cite{[CITE:Good2018]}, gene expression
signatures~\cite{[CITE:Fitter2021]}, DNA methylation
arrays~\cite{[CITE:Mosquera2024]}, and electronic medical
records~\cite{[CITE:Pan2017]} have demonstrated improved risk
separation beyond standard criteria.
Nevertheless, two fundamental limitations persist across this
literature.
First, most methods operate on a \emph{fixed, single-timepoint}
feature snapshot at diagnosis, ignoring the structured temporal
ordering in which clinical data actually become available—molecular
cytogenetics results are routinely unavailable until Day~7--10, and
MRD until Day~19--46.
A model trained on features pooled from all timepoints may implicitly
rely on information that would not be available at the point of
clinical decision-making, producing optimistic but
non-deployable estimates~\cite{[CITE:Steyerberg2019]}.
Second, standard train-test splits shuffle patients
chronologically, whereas in practice a hospital's knowledge base
grows year by year; temporal validation is required to confirm
that predictive performance generalises to truly \emph{prospective}
patients.
Computational flow cytometry approaches have further shown that
immunophenotypic data at diagnosis alone may be insufficient for
relapse prediction~\cite{[CITE:Martinez2025]}, highlighting the need
for multimodal and longitudinal integration.

The emergence of large language models (LLMs) with strong zero-shot
reasoning capabilities opens a complementary avenue: given structured
clinical notes or reports describing a patient's feature profile, an
LLM may distil prognostic patterns encoded in its pre-training without
requiring labelled examples from the target
cohort~\cite{[CITE:Phaterpekar2026]}.
However, systematic evaluations of LLM zero-shot performance on
paediatric oncology outcome prediction—particularly for future events
such as relapse and survival—remain absent from the literature.
Equally unexplored is whether LLM-based risk scores can serve as
survival predictors comparable to purpose-built survival models.

Here we address these gaps with a cohort of 301 Chinese paediatric ALL
patients and a \emph{temporally-structured} prediction framework.
Our contributions are: (\textit{i})~a twelve-level feature set
hierarchy $\mathcal{F}_1 \subseteq \cdots \subseteq \mathcal{F}_{12}$
whose boundaries align exactly with the clinical data-acquisition
timeline (Day~0 through Day~46+); (\textit{ii})~Phase~1, a systematic
zero-shot evaluation of four LLMs across all feature levels and six
prediction targets, establishing performance baselines without any
model training; (\textit{iii})~Phase~2, a decision tree and random
survival forest system trained on a dynamic case database partitioned
by patient enrolment year, validating performance under three temporal
splits that mimic real-world database growth; (\textit{iv})~a bone
marrow morphology pipeline converting microscopy images to structured
text reports via SAM-based cell segmentation and BiomedCLIP clustering,
which augments LLM inputs; and (\textit{v})~a RAG component that retrieves the $K=5$ most similar
historical cases to (\textit{a})~perform a similarity-weighted belief
revision of the RF predicted probability (improving AUROC on D46MRD
and D19MRD tasks) and (\textit{b})~generate clinically interpretable
LLM narratives grounding each prediction in historical precedent.

Central to our framework is a twelve-level feature hierarchy
$\mathcal{F}_1 \subseteq \mathcal{F}_2 \subseteq \cdots \subseteq
\mathcal{F}_{12}$ (Table~\ref{tab:expdesign}), where each level
$\mathcal{F}_k$ corresponds to features available at clinical
timepoint~$\tau_k$.
$\mathcal{F}_1$ contains only white blood cell count and age—information
present at the moment of hospital admission—while $\mathcal{F}_{8}$
additionally includes Day~19 and Day~46 MRD values obtained at the
end of induction chemotherapy.
Feature sets with image-derived inputs ($\mathcal{F}_4$, $\mathcal{F}_{11}$,
$\mathcal{F}_{12}$) further incorporate bone marrow morphology in the
form of either raw microscopy tiles or structured text reports produced
by our automated pipeline (Section~\ref{sec:morph}).
This design ensures that any model trained or evaluated at level~$k$
uses only information that would be accessible in clinical practice at
timepoint~$\tau_k$, avoiding temporal leakage.

\subsection{Phase~1: LLM zero-shot prediction across the feature timeline}
\label{sec:phase1}
%%--------------------------------------------------------------%%

We evaluated four LLMs in a zero-shot setting across all twelve feature
levels and four prediction targets, yielding a systematic picture of
how predictive performance evolves as more clinical information becomes
available (Fig.~\ref{fig:ablation}).
Models included a proprietary cloud model (Gemini-3.1-Flash-Lite) and
three open-weight models deployed locally via vLLM on dual A100~80G
GPUs: Gemma-4-31B-it, InternVL3.5-30B-A3B, and Qwen3.6-35B-A3B.
Each patient's feature vector was serialised into natural language and
presented to the model with a task-specific system prompt
(Methods, Section~\ref{sec:llm_inference}); the model output a
categorical label or a continuous risk score depending on the task.

\textbf{Final Risk stratification.}
At Day~0 ($\mathcal{F}_1$, WBC and age only), Gemini and Gemma already
achieved macro-averaged AUROC values of approximately 0.65--0.70 on
Final Risk, reflecting the known prognostic importance of WBC count.
Performance increased incrementally through $\mathcal{F}_3$ (flow
cytometry panel) and $\mathcal{F}_6$--$\mathcal{F}_7$ (cytogenetics
and initial risk classification), with Gemma reaching AUROC~= 0.885
at $\mathcal{F}_{10}$ (full Day~7--10 features plus diagnostic report).
The addition of MRD values at $\mathcal{F}_8$ produced the largest
single improvement across all feature levels, with Gemma achieving
AUROC~= 0.885 and Gemini AUROC~= 0.842 at this level.
These results confirm that MRD is the dominant signal for final
risk classification, consistent with clinical guidelines.

\textbf{Relapse prediction.}
Relapse prediction proved substantially harder for all models and
feature levels.
Across $\mathcal{F}_1$--$\mathcal{F}_{12}$, AUROC values for relapse
ranged from approximately 0.50 to 0.59 across the four LLMs, with no
single feature set or model achieving consistent improvement.
The best results were obtained with morphology-augmented feature sets:
Gemma reached AUROC~= 0.585 at $\mathcal{F}_{4.1}$ (blast morphology
text report added to $\mathcal{F}_3$), and similar improvements of
0.017--0.036 AUROC over raw image inputs were observed across all
four models (Fig.~\ref{fig:morph}), indicating that structured
morphological statistics convey information about relapse biology
beyond what raw tiles provide to LLMs.

\textbf{MRD prediction.}
LLMs showed moderate performance on D19MRD and D46MRD prediction
(AUROC~0.60--0.80) using pre-MRD features only, suggesting that the
models can partially infer early treatment response from the
immunophenotypic and cytogenetic profile alone.

\textbf{Comparative model behaviour.}
Gemini and Gemma consistently outperformed InternVL and Qwen on most
tasks; notably, Qwen3.6-35B-A3B showed near-chance AUROC on D19/D46MRD
and Relapse despite competitive performance on Final Risk, suggesting
a tendency to predict only the majority class for highly imbalanced
tasks.
InternVL performed comparably on Final Risk (AUROC~$\approx$~0.58--0.66)
but showed weaker text-comprehension of structured feature reports.

%%--------------------------------------------------------------%%
\subsection{Bone marrow morphology pipeline}
\label{sec:morph_results}
%%--------------------------------------------------------------%%

Bone marrow smear images were processed by an automated four-stage
pipeline: SAM-based cell segmentation, morphological feature extraction
($\sim$37 features per cell including area, circularity,
nucleus-to-cytoplasm ratio, and GLCM texture), BiomedCLIP zero-shot
clustering ($k=7$ with Hungarian assignment to cell types), and
structured text report generation (Methods, Section~\ref{sec:morph}).
This converts heterogeneous microscopy data into a consistent natural
language representation accessible to LLMs.

Comparing $\mathcal{F}_{4}$ (raw images directly provided to
multimodal LLMs) with $\mathcal{F}_{4.1}$ (blast-only morphology text
report substituted for images), the text-report approach achieved
higher Relapse AUROC for all four models (improvements of
+0.017~Gemini, +0.035~Gemma, +0.022~InternVL, +0.036~Qwen), while
Final Risk AUROC remained comparable or slightly improved.
The all-cell-type report ($\mathcal{F}_{4.2}$) improved Final Risk for
Gemma (AUROC~0.854 vs 0.840 for raw images) but was less beneficial
for Relapse, suggesting that blast-specific features are more
informative for predicting treatment failure.
These results demonstrate that domain-specific cell-level
characterisation provides predictive signal complementary to global
image appearance.

%%--------------------------------------------------------------%%
\subsection{Phase~2: Temporal validation with a dynamic case database}
\label{sec:phase2}
%%--------------------------------------------------------------%%

Phase~2 evaluates whether predictive performance is maintained under a
realistic deployment scenario in which a model can only be trained on
patients enrolled before the prediction date.
We defined three temporal splits based on patient enrolment year
$\tau_i$:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{D}_s^{\text{train}} = \{i : \tau_i \leq \mathcal{T}_s\},
\quad
\mathcal{D}_s^{\text{test}} = \{i : \tau_i > \mathcal{T}_s\},
\label{eq:splits}
\end{equation}

where $\mathcal{T}_1 = 2016$ ($n_{\text{train}}=99$,
$n_{\text{test}}=201$), $\mathcal{T}_2 = 2017$
($n_{\text{train}}=186$, $n_{\text{test}}=114$), and
$\mathcal{T}_3 = 2018$ ($n_{\text{train}}=283$, $n_{\text{test}}=17$).
All preprocessing statistics (means, standard deviations for
z-scoring; one-hot encoding maps) were derived exclusively from
$\mathcal{D}_s^{\text{train}}$ and applied to $\mathcal{D}_s^{\text{test}}$,
ensuring strict temporal non-leakage.
A decision tree classifier (DT; max depth~4, class-weight balanced) and
random forest (RF; $n_{\text{estimators}}=100$) were trained for each
feature level and split combination; we report DT as the primary model
for interpretability and RF as an ensemble upper bound.

\textbf{Final Risk — ablation across feature levels (Split~2).}
Figure~\ref{fig:p2ablation} shows the DT AUROC for Final Risk
across the feature hierarchy on Split~2 ($n_{\text{test}}=114$, the
most statistically robust split).
Performance rose from AUROC~=~0.810 at $\mathcal{F}_1$ (WBC, age) to
AUROC~=~0.912 at $\mathcal{F}_7$ (full cytogenetic panel), with the
largest gain occurring at $\mathcal{F}_6$ (+0.127 AUROC, reflecting
the introduction of Initial Risk and Day~5 peripheral blast percentage
into the feature set).
Including MRD at $\mathcal{F}_8$ further raised DT AUROC to
\textbf{0.949} (RF: 0.998), the highest performance across all
experimental conditions.
The RF AUC approaching 1.0 at $\mathcal{F}_8$ is consistent with the
known clinical rule—patients with undetectable MRD at Day~46 are
overwhelmingly low-risk—and should be interpreted as a sanity check
rather than a novel discovery.
The DT, constrained to depth~4, provides a more conservative and
clinically transferable estimate.

\textbf{Temporal learning curve.}
Across Split~1 ($n_{\text{train}}=99$) to Split~2
($n_{\text{train}}=186$), Final Risk DT AUROC increased from [X] to
0.949, consistent with the intuition that a growing case database
enables better discrimination.
Split~3 ($n_{\text{test}}=17$) is underpowered for most tasks and is
reported with 95\% bootstrap confidence intervals in
Supplementary Table~S1.

\textbf{Relapse prediction.}
Relapse prediction remained challenging for DT and RF alike (best
DT AUROC~= 0.529, Split~2), mirroring the difficulty observed in
Phase~1 and corroborating findings from
recent computational flow cytometry studies~\cite{[CITE:Martinez2025]}.
This limitation likely reflects the multifactorial biology of
relapse—involving treatment adherence, minimal residual disease
kinetics, and late-emerging clonal evolution—that may not be
fully captured by the features available in our cohort.

%%--------------------------------------------------------------%%
\subsection{Survival prediction}
\label{sec:survival}
%%--------------------------------------------------------------%%

We evaluated risk stratification for event-free survival (EFS; 75
events: 45 relapses, 23 induction failures, 7 deaths) and overall
survival (OS; 24 deaths) using two complementary approaches.
In Phase~1, LLM-derived risk scores (continuous values 0--1 output by
the system prompt requesting event probability) were used directly as
survival predictors evaluated via Harrell's concordance
index~(\ref{eq:cindex}) and IPCW time-dependent
AUC~(\ref{eq:itauc}).
In Phase~2, a random survival forest (RSF) was trained on temporally
partitioned data.

\textbf{EFS.}
Phase~1 LLMs achieved peak EFS concordance indices of 0.716 (Gemma,
$\mathcal{F}_8$), 0.705 (Gemini, $\mathcal{F}_9$), 0.694
(Qwen, $\mathcal{F}_9$), and 0.682 (InternVL, $\mathcal{F}_9$)
across all feature levels.
The MRD-only feature set $\mathcal{F}_9$ was consistently optimal or
near-optimal for LLM-based EFS prediction, confirming that residual
disease burden encodes strong prognostic information accessible to
LLMs without any exposure to labelled survival data.
Phase~2 RSF on Split~2 attained C-index~= \textbf{0.746}
(time-AUC at 24~months~= 0.775, log-rank $p = 0.010$) at
$\mathcal{F}_8$, a modest but consistent improvement over the
best Phase~1 LLM.

\textbf{Kaplan-Meier risk stratification.}
For each model and feature level, patients were dichotomised at the
median predicted risk score and Kaplan-Meier curves were estimated for
the two strata (Fig.~\ref{fig:km}).
Significant group separation (log-rank $p < 0.05$) was achieved by
all four LLMs at $\mathcal{F}_8$/$\mathcal{F}_9$, and by the
Phase~2 RSF at $\mathcal{F}_8$ (Split~2, $p = 0.010$).

\textbf{OS.}
With only 24 OS events (8.0\% event rate), statistical power for OS
models is limited; C-indices ranged from 0.629 to 0.772 across
models and feature levels, with no consistent ordering.
InternVL reached C-index~= 0.772 at $\mathcal{F}_{11}$ (C-index at
24~months AUC~= 0.762, $p = 0.0001$); however, results at this
feature level incorporate follow-up imaging and should be interpreted
with caution regarding temporal availability.
Phase~2 RSF Split~3 OS results are omitted due to zero OS events in
the 17-patient 2019 test set.
A larger cohort or multi-centre study would be required to draw
robust conclusions about OS.

%%--------------------------------------------------------------%%
\subsection{Interpretable prediction via retrieval-augmented generation}
\label{sec:rag}
%%--------------------------------------------------------------%%

To provide clinically grounded explanations and quantitative
probability corrections for Phase~2 predictions, we implemented a
RAG component that, for each test patient $i$, retrieves the $K=5$
most similar training cases by cosine similarity in the
training-standardised feature space (Eq.~\ref{eq:rag}), applies a
belief revision to the RF predicted probability, and then generates
an LLM narrative (Methods, Section~\ref{sec:rag_methods}).

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{N}(i) = \underset{j \in \mathcal{D}_s^{\text{train}}}
  {\arg\text{top-}K}\
  \frac{\mathbf{z}_i \cdot \mathbf{z}_j}
       {\|\mathbf{z}_i\|_2 \|\mathbf{z}_j\|_2},
\quad K = 5.
\label{eq:rag}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Belief revision results.}
Table~\ref{tab:belief_revision} reports AUROC before and after belief
revision across the four classification tasks on representative splits.
Belief revision improved AUROC on Final~Risk (Split~2:
$0.998 \to 0.999$), D19MRD (Split~2: $0.767 \to 0.779$;
Split~3: $0.833 \to 0.857$), and most substantially on D46MRD
(Split~2: $0.533 \to 0.579$; Split~3: $0.655 \to 0.881$, $\Delta =
+0.226$).
The large gain on D46MRD/Split~3 reflects a high-quality retrieval
regime: with 283 training cases, the five nearest neighbours share
strong phenotypic similarity (mean cosine~$\bar{s} \approx 0.91$),
and their D46MRD outcomes are highly concordant with the query patient.
By contrast, belief revision reduced AUROC for Relapse (Split~2:
$0.630 \to 0.588$, $\Delta = -0.042$): because relapse prediction is
intrinsically noisy (best RF AUROC~$\approx 0.63$), retrieved neighbours
have mixed outcomes, and the RAG evidence acts as noise rather than
a corrective signal.
This finding delineates a boundary condition for belief revision: it
benefits tasks where the feature space clusters meaningfully with
respect to the outcome (Final~Risk, MRD), but introduces noise for
tasks with poor class separation in feature space (Relapse).

%%==============================================================%%
\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}
%%==============================================================%%

This study presents a temporally-structured multimodal framework for
paediatric ALL outcome prediction that explicitly mirrors the clinical
data-acquisition workflow, enabling meaningful predictions at each
decision node from admission to end-of-induction.
Several key findings warrant discussion.

\textbf{MRD as the dominant predictor.}
The most striking result across both Phase~1 and Phase~2 is the
predictive jump attributable to MRD values: DT AUROC for Final Risk
increased from 0.912 ($\mathcal{F}_7$, cytogenetics) to 0.949
($\mathcal{F}_8$, plus D19/D46 MRD) under Split~2 conditions.
Similarly, LLM EFS C-index peaked at feature levels that included
MRD.
This quantitatively confirms the central role of MRD in the
clinical risk-stratification guideline~\cite{[CITE:Conter2010]}, and
provides an AUROC estimate that directly characterises the
discriminative value of adding MRD to cytogenetic profiling.
From a clinical translation perspective, the DT achieves 0.949 AUROC
for Final Risk with a transparent depth-4 decision tree that can be
directly audited by clinicians, which is important for institutional
adoption~\cite{[CITE:Rudin2019]}.

\textbf{Temporal validation and database growth.}
The consistent improvement in Final Risk AUROC as the training
database grows from 99 (Split~1) to 186 (Split~2) patients suggests
that accumulating historical cases continues to benefit predictive
performance, motivating a prospective case-database design for
clinical AI systems.
The smallest test set (Split~3, $n=17$) also yielded the highest
nominal DT AUROC (0.979), likely an artefact of the small and
possibly unrepresentative 2019 cohort rather than genuine improvement,
underscoring the importance of interpreting results from small test
sets with bootstrap confidence intervals.

\textbf{LLM zero-shot as a competitive baseline.}
Phase~1 LLMs reached Final Risk AUROC values of 0.842--0.885 without
any labelled training data, a result competitive with the Phase~2 DT
($\leq \mathcal{F}_7$ conditions).
This indicates that the clinical knowledge encoded during LLM
pre-training includes a meaningful understanding of paediatric ALL risk
factors.
LLMs may therefore be particularly valuable in settings where
historical case databases are small or absent—for example, at the
time a new treatment protocol is introduced—serving as a strong
prior until sufficient labelled data accumulate.

\textbf{The challenge of relapse prediction.}
Relapse prediction proved intractable for both LLMs (AUROC $\approx$
0.50--0.59) and DT/RF (best DT AUROC~= 0.529), consistent with the
broader literature~\cite{[CITE:Martinez2025],[CITE:Good2018]}.
Our feature set does not include genomic or epigenomic data (e.g.,
copy-number variation, DNA methylation), which have shown modest
incremental utility in single-centre studies~\cite{[CITE:Mosquera2024]}.
Future work integrating multi-omics data within the temporal feature
hierarchy may improve relapse prediction.

\textbf{Boundary conditions for RAG belief revision.}
Our results reveal that similarity-weighted belief revision delivers
quantitative gains only when the feature space clusters meaningfully
by the target label.
For D46MRD (Split~3: AUROC $0.655 \to 0.881$, $\Delta{+}0.226$) and
D19MRD, the retrieved top-5 neighbours share concordant outcomes,
providing reliable evidence that tightens the RF probability.
For Final Risk the RF baseline is already near-ceiling ($\text{AUROC}
\approx 1.0$), so the revision is marginal.
For Relapse—where RF AUROC $\approx 0.53$--$0.63$ signals poor class
separation—retrieved neighbours have heterogeneous outcomes and the
RAG evidence degrades performance ($\Delta -0.042$ on Split~2).
The same pattern holds for survival: the OS event rate (8\%) means
that training neighbours are predominantly censored, driving
$p_{\text{rag}} \to 0$ and introducing noise.
These findings suggest a practical deployment criterion: compute the
mean inter-neighbour outcome agreement (e.g., Fleiss $\kappa$ over
top-$K$ retrieved labels) and apply belief revision only when
agreement exceeds a calibrated threshold, defaulting to the raw model
probability otherwise.

\textbf{Limitations.}
This is a single-centre retrospective study; external validation in
independent cohorts is required before clinical deployment.
The relatively small cohort ($n=301$) limits the statistical power for
OS analysis (24 events) and for the smallest temporal split ($n=17$).
All Phase~1 LLM experiments used zero-shot prompting; fine-tuning on
labelled clinical notes from this or similar cohorts may yield
further improvements.
External baseline comparisons against published methods trained on
independent datasets have not yet been performed and are a planned
extension of this work.

\section{Methods}\label{sec:methods}
%%==============================================================%%

\subsection{Dataset and ethics}\label{sec:dataset}

The cohort comprised 301 patients diagnosed with acute lymphoblastic
leukaemia at [HOSPITAL NAME] between 2015 and 2019.
All patients were treated with risk-stratified chemotherapy protocols
consistent with contemporary Chinese paediatric oncology
guidelines.
Ethics approval was obtained from [ETHICS NUMBER]; patient data were
de-identified prior to analysis.
For each patient the dataset includes:
(i) structured clinical data (demographics, haematological indices,
immunophenotype, 51-marker flow cytometry panel, cytogenetics,
MRD values);
(ii) bone marrow aspirate microscopy images (Giemsa-stained smear,
$\sim$20 fields per patient, total [X]~images);
(iii) diagnostic and follow-up bone marrow pathology text reports.

\subsection{Temporally-structured feature design}\label{sec:features}

We define feature sets as a strictly nested sequence
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{F}_1 \subseteq \mathcal{F}_2 \subseteq \cdots \subseteq
\mathcal{F}_{12},
\label{eq:featseq}
\end{equation}
where each $\mathcal{F}_k$ contains all features clinically available
at timepoint $\tau_k$.
Boundaries were established by consulting the standard ALL treatment
protocol: $\mathcal{F}_1$~(Day~0): WBC count and age;
$\mathcal{F}_2$~(Day~1): adds blast percentage from bone marrow
aspirate; $\mathcal{F}_3$~(Day~1): adds immunophenotype classification
and 51 flow cytometry surface markers (42 CD antigens and 9 other
markers including cMPO, TdT, and HLADR); $\mathcal{F}_6$~(Day~5):
adds CNS involvement status, Day~5 peripheral blast percentage, and
Initial Risk classification; $\mathcal{F}_7$~(Day~7--10): adds fusion
gene, karyotype, and FISH results; $\mathcal{F}_8$~(Day~46): adds
D19MRD and D46MRD numerical values.
Image-based feature sets $\mathcal{F}_{4}$, $\mathcal{F}_{4.1}$,
$\mathcal{F}_{4.2}$, $\mathcal{F}_{11}$, and $\mathcal{F}_{12.x}$
incorporate bone marrow morphology as described in
Section~\ref{sec:morph}.

For each patient $i$, the input vector at level $k$ is
$\mathbf{x}_i^{(k)} \in \mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{F}_k|}$.
Categorical features (Immunophenotype, Fusion Gene) were encoded by
natural-language description for LLM inputs, and by one-hot encoding
for DT/RF inputs with mappings derived exclusively from the training
split (Section~\ref{sec:dt}).

\subsection{Bone marrow morphology pipeline}\label{sec:morph}

Each bone marrow aspirate image was processed through a four-stage
automated pipeline.

\textbf{Stage 1 — Cell segmentation.}
We applied SAM~3.1~\cite{[CITE:SAM]} with a text prompt
(\textit{``complete dark-stained nucleated cell''}) to each image
after colour-based pre-filtering (HSV purple range 120--160°) to
restrict prompt sampling to haematoxylin-stained regions.
Segmented cell patches were filtered by darkness ratio ($<0.82$)
and minimum stained pixel fraction ($>0.05$).

\textbf{Stage 2 — Morphological feature extraction.}
For each retained cell, $\sim$37 features were computed from the
segmentation mask and corresponding RGB patch, including geometric
descriptors (area, perimeter, circularity, eccentricity, solidity,
major/minor axis, aspect ratio), textural descriptors (GLCM
contrast, correlation, energy at multiple angles), nucleus-to-cytoplasm
(N/C) ratio, nucleoli count, and vacuole percentage.

\textbf{Stage 3 — Cell-type classification.}
BiomedCLIP~\cite{[CITE:BiomedCLIP]} was applied in a zero-shot
clustering mode with $k=7$ prototype classes (blast, granulocyte,
megakaryocyte, monocyte, plasma cell, red cell, other).
Hungarian algorithm assignment mapped cluster indices to cell-type
labels using cosine similarity to BiomedCLIP text embeddings.

\textbf{Stage 4 — Report generation.}
Per-patient statistics (cell counts, mean morphological features per
class, blast proportion) were aggregated across images and serialised
into a structured natural-language report supplied as additional
context to LLM prompts in $\mathcal{F}_{4.1}$, $\mathcal{F}_{4.2}$,
$\mathcal{F}_{10}$, $\mathcal{F}_{12.1}$.

\subsection{Phase~1: LLM zero-shot inference}\label{sec:llm_inference}

Four LLMs were evaluated. Gemini-3.1-Flash-Lite was accessed via the
Google AI API.
Gemma-4-31B-it, InternVL3.5-30B-A3B, and Qwen3.6-35B-A3B were
deployed locally with vLLM~\cite{[CITE:vLLM]} using tensor parallelism
across two A100~80G GPUs.

For classification tasks (D19MRD, D46MRD, Final Risk, Relapse), each
patient's features were serialised into a key--value list and
submitted with a task-specific system prompt instructing the model to
output a bracketed label (e.g., \texttt{[[Positive]]} /
\texttt{[[Negative]]}).
For survival risk scoring, the model output a continuous probability
(0.0--1.0) of an adverse event, parsed by extracting the first
numerical token and clipping to $[0, 1]$.
All experiments used temperature~$= 0$ (greedy decoding) to ensure
reproducibility.
Per-patient outputs were cached; the full cohort of 301 patients was
scored for each combination of feature level, task, and model,
yielding $12 \times 4 \times 4 = 192$ experimental cells for
classification and $12 \times 2 \times 4 = 96$ for survival scoring.

\subsection{Phase~2: Decision tree and random forest with
  temporal splits}\label{sec:dt}

For each temporal split $s$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:splits}) and feature level $k$,
numerical features were z-score normalised using training-set
statistics only:
\begin{equation}
z_{ij}^{(\text{test})} =
\frac{x_{ij}^{(\text{test})} - \mu_j^{(\text{train})}}
     {\sigma_j^{(\text{train})}},
\quad j = 1, \ldots, |\mathcal{F}_k|.
\label{eq:zscore}
\end{equation}
Categorical features used one-hot encoding with maps derived from
the training set.
Missing values were imputed with the training-set median per feature.

A decision tree classifier was trained with
\texttt{max\_depth}~=~4, \texttt{class\_weight}~=~`balanced',
\texttt{criterion}~=~`gini', and \texttt{random\_state}~=~42.
The Gini impurity at node~$t$ is
\begin{equation}
G(t) = 1 - \sum_{k=1}^{K} \hat{p}_{tk}^2,
\label{eq:gini}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{p}_{tk}$ is the empirical class proportion.
A random forest with $n_{\text{estimators}}=100$ and
\texttt{class\_weight}~=~`balanced' was trained in parallel as an
ensemble upper bound; RF results are reported in supplementary tables.

Primary evaluation metric is AUROC; F1-macro and balanced accuracy
are reported as secondary metrics.
Bootstrap 95\% confidence intervals (1000 resamples) are reported for
Split~3 ($n_{\text{test}}=17$).

\subsection{Phase~2: RAG retrieval, belief revision, and LLM
narration}\label{sec:rag_methods}

For a test patient $i$, the $K=5$ most similar training cases were
retrieved by cosine similarity in the training-standardised feature
space (Eq.~\ref{eq:rag}).

\textbf{Belief revision.}
Denote the RF predicted probability for patient $i$ as $p_0$.
Given the retrieved neighbours $\mathcal{N}(i) = \{j_1,\ldots,j_K\}$
with cosine similarities $s_{j}$ and binary task outcomes
$y_j \in \{0, 1\}$, the RAG evidence probability is the
similarity-weighted outcome mean:
\begin{equation}
p_{\text{rag}} =
  \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}(i)} s_j\, y_j}
       {\displaystyle\sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}(i)} s_j}.
\label{eq:prag}
\end{equation}
The revised probability interpolates between the model prior and the
RAG evidence, with the evidence weight scaling with the mean
similarity $\bar{s} = \frac{1}{K}\sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}(i)} s_j$:
\begin{equation}
p_{\text{rev}} = \alpha\, p_0 + (1-\alpha)\, p_{\text{rag}},
\qquad
\alpha = 1 - (1-\alpha_{\text{base}})\sqrt{\bar{s}},
\label{eq:prev}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_{\text{base}} = 0.6$.
When retrieved cases are highly similar ($\bar{s}\to 1$), the evidence
weight $(1-\alpha) \to 0.4$; when similarity is low, the original RF
probability is preserved.
For survival prediction, $p_0$ is replaced by the min-max normalised
RSF risk score $r_{\text{norm}} \in [0,1]$, and $y_j = \delta_j$
(the event indicator of each training neighbour).

\textbf{LLM narration.}
After belief revision, an LLM prompt was constructed containing:
(i) the patient's feature values, (ii) the RF-predicted label and
$p_{\text{rev}}$ with the human-readable DT decision path (split
conditions from root to leaf), (iii) the top-6 patient-salient
features (ranked by $\text{importance}_j \times |z_{ij}|$), and
(iv) the structured profiles of the top-5 retrieved cases including
their actual outcomes.
The model was instructed to produce a 3--5 sentence clinical
narrative explaining the prediction.
LLM narratives are produced by Gemini-3.1-Flash-Lite and are used
exclusively for qualitative case-study presentation; they do not
contribute to any quantitative evaluation.

\subsection{Survival analysis}\label{sec:surv_methods}

EFS was defined as time (months) from diagnosis to first of:
relapse, induction failure, or death, with censoring at last
follow-up.
OS was defined as time from diagnosis to death, censored at last
follow-up.

Phase~1 continuous risk scores $\hat{r}_i \in [0,1]$ were treated as
survival predictors.
Harrell's concordance index is
\begin{equation}
\hat{C} =
\frac{
  \displaystyle\sum_{i \neq j}
  \mathbf{1}[T_i < T_j]\,
  \mathbf{1}[\delta_i = 1]\,
  \mathbf{1}[\hat{r}_i > \hat{r}_j]
}{
  \displaystyle\sum_{i \neq j}
  \mathbf{1}[T_i < T_j]\,
  \mathbf{1}[\delta_i = 1]
},
\label{eq:cindex}
\end{equation}
where $T_i$ is follow-up time, $\delta_i \in \{0,1\}$ is the event
indicator.

Time-dependent AUC at time $t^*$ was computed using inverse
probability of censoring weighting (IPCW)~\cite{[CITE:Uno2011]}:
\begin{equation}
\widehat{\text{AUC}}(t^*) =
\frac{
  \displaystyle\sum_{i:\,T_i \leq t^*,\,\delta_i=1}
  \sum_{j:\,T_j > t^*}
  \mathbf{1}[\hat{r}_i > \hat{r}_j]\,\hat{w}_i
}{
  \displaystyle\sum_{i:\,T_i \leq t^*,\,\delta_i=1}
  \sum_{j:\,T_j > t^*}
  \hat{w}_i
},
\label{eq:itauc}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{w}_i = 1/\hat{G}(T_i)^2$ and $\hat{G}(\cdot)$ is the
Kaplan-Meier estimator of the censoring distribution.
Time points evaluated: $t^* \in \{12, 24, 36\}$ months.

Kaplan-Meier curves were estimated for high- and low-risk groups
(dichotomised at the median predicted risk score):
\begin{equation}
\hat{S}(t) = \prod_{t_i \leq t}
\left(1 - \frac{d_i}{n_i}\right),
\label{eq:km}
\end{equation}
with group separation assessed by the log-rank test.

Phase~2 survival used a random survival forest
(RSF;~\cite{[CITE:Ishwaran2008]}; $n_{\text{trees}}=100$) with the
same temporal train/test splits as the classification experiments;
evaluation used identical C-index and IPCW time-AUC metrics.

\subsection{Statistical analysis}

AUROC was computed with the \texttt{scikit-learn}
library~\cite{[CITE:sklearn]}; C-index and IPCW AUC with
\texttt{scikit-survival}~\cite{[CITE:sksurv]}.
No multiple testing correction was applied given the exploratory
nature of this study; all reported $p$-values are two-sided.
